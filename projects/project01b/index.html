Hallucinations

A Personal
Examination

Overview

Hallucinations is a shot on video movie about three brothers left home alone while their mom is stuck working through the night. After one brother meets the nextdoor monk, the three begin to experience increasingly violent and deadly hallucinations. The film stars John Polonia, Mark Polonia, and Todd Michael Smith. The three of them are also the directors, cinematographers, etc...
Shot on video (SOV) films are films shot directly on consumer available camcorders. During the 80's, many artists utilized these goods to film their own movies and distribute them to as many video stores as possible. Essentially, homemade movies were able to be mass produced and consumed by fellow common people. Horror was essentially the only genre prominent in this movement, as tons of creatives would film their own shock/horror films at home with no budget.
I was sucked in based on the cover (to the right), which has some pretty cool artwork.

The lack of a budget or acting ability is a prominent part of this film. Props are (in a way, charmingly) handmade, imperfect things that are constructed out of household materials. It's impossible to have a scene with all three brothers, as one always has to be filming/directing. Any gore is instantly cut away and returned to to avoid the need for mechanical effects.
Despite this, there's a clear passion. Scenes where they try and portray throwing up organs (a pink, tied up t-shirt) or a bizarre worm creature stalks the shower (an old ribbed pipe with some clay at the end). It's easy to have your nonexistent budget control your scene choices, but that doesn't happen here.

Review

Objectively, this is a terrible film. The dialogue is hard to understand, the acting is horrible, the props are cheap... And really, not much happens beyond continually weird hallucinations from the brothers. Characters are nonexistent beyond the defining trait of one brother being adopted and another being mentally handicapped, and I can't remember a single name. There are some wins, though.
A film where passion is clearly put into it is more enjoyable than another lifeless straight to DVD piece of garbage. Even if scenes end up being corny, it's enjoyable to see three fans of horror create something they clearly care about. The music is also quite effective in being eerie. Scenes where things would be objectively funny (a shaking clay human that looks nothing lifelike) end up so bizarre that you get uncomfortable among music made from slowed down growing and dog whining.

So why...

For some reason, after I finished watching this film, it wouldn't leave my head. Not in a way that reflected it had truly scared me, like some films of the past had. Frankly, I don't think anything in this movie is objectively scary aside from concepts. I eventually realized, I was so struck with this movie based on the love the brothers put into it.
See, on the left, a homage to Freddy Krueger composed of a tracksuit, hat, and some paper mache.

I have a deep love for the homemade world of content. Be it something physically burned onto a DVD, or posted just online. This film struck a sentimental chord in me, of people unafraid to put themselves out there and make a pure passion project out of nothing but an adoration for horror. I love when people create, regardless of experience or budget. Even if I tend to watch those types of things for a kick of humor, it's out of a love for human creation. Creativity stomps out objects blocking the production of something great, and this film really showcases that if you want to make something - you should just do it.

The Uncanny and Bizarre

I think this movie really stuck with me not just for its very existence, but for the absolute absurdity of it. I enjoy art, but I wouldn't consider myself someone into avant-garde films or anything insanely focused on form. There is something incredibly fascinating to me about bizarre artwork, that I think (at the risk of sounding like a hipster) it showcases human nature in a weird way (because isn't it in itself absurd that we even exist as a species?).
This movie reminded me of what it means to create as a human, and the capabilities of weirdness we hide in our brains.

Staying Weird

I've heard the term "weird for the sake of being weird" thrown around as an insult before, which I despise! Weird artwork and videos are an opportunity for people to make whatever comes to mind, to really showcase any creative thought in their brain.
Primus' music video, right beside this panel, contains bizarre rubbery cowboys that have absolutely nothing to do with what they're singing about.

Creation is beautiful!

To showcase the beauty of the absurd, I took a screenshot of my liked videos. Something I will forever adore about the internet is the fact I have access to all the weird little things people feel like sharing, and I loathe the idea that one day people will be too scared of embarrassing themselves to create.
